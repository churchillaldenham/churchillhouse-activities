British Museum

8 million objects. One afternoon. Here's what not to miss.
Top 10 60+ Galleries Every Continent Self-Guided

The World Under One Roof

The British Museum was the first national public museum in the world. When it opened in 1759, anyone could enter for free — a radical idea at a time when great collections were exclusively for the wealthy. That principle hasn't changed. Entry is still free, and the collection has grown to over 8 million objects from every culture and every era of human history.

Unlike most excursions, there is no guided tour. Students explore at their own pace across the 60 galleries, moving from ancient Egypt to the Americas to Japan in minutes.

The Round Reading Room

Karl Marx wrote parts of Das Kapital here. Oscar Wilde and Arthur Conan Doyle used it as a workspace. It was once one of the most important reading rooms in Europe, tucked inside the Great Court before the glass roof was added in 2000. The Great Court itself is now the largest covered public square in Europe.

🗺️

No Set Route

Unlike many museums, the British Museum has no single recommended path. Students can explore like a maze, discovering unexpected objects along the way. The Enlightenment Gallery in Room 1 is a good starting point — it recreates the feel of an 18th-century collector's world and sets up the rest of the visit beautifully.

Rosetta Stone
01Room 4 — Egyptian Sculpture
▾
Why It Matters
"For 1,400 years, nobody on Earth could read ancient Egyptian hieroglyphics. This stone changed that."

The Rosetta Stone was carved in 196 BC and carries the same text written in three scripts: hieroglyphic, demotic and ancient Greek. Scholars could already read Greek. By comparing the three versions, they were able to decode hieroglyphics for the first time in over a millennium, unlocking the entire written history of ancient Egypt. It is one of the most significant archaeological discoveries ever made and is usually the most visited object in the museum.

🏛️
Parthenon Sculptures
02Room 18 — Greece: Athens
▾
Why It Matters
"These sculptures were carved in the 5th century BC. They are also at the centre of one of the most contested debates in the museum world today."

The marble sculptures once decorated the Parthenon temple in Athens, created around 447 BC under the direction of the sculptor Pheidias. They show gods, heroes and scenes from Greek mythology with a level of detail and naturalism that was revolutionary at the time. The Greek government has requested their return for decades, arguing they belong in Athens alongside the rest of the temple's surviving decoration. The British Museum has so far refused. The debate about who owns ancient artefacts, and where they belong, is one of the most significant conversations in modern archaeology.

💬 Talking Point "Should these sculptures be here, or in Athens? They were created for a specific building in a specific city. Does it matter that they can be seen by more people in London? Does the fact that they've been here for over 200 years change anything?"
🏺
Crouching Venus
03Room 15 — Roman Empire
▾
Why It Matters
"This statue is a Roman copy of a Greek original that no longer exists. The copy is over 1,800 years old."

Dating from the 2nd century AD, this marble figure shows the goddess Aphrodite surprised at her bath — a pose that became enormously influential in European art over the following centuries. The original Greek sculpture it was based on has been lost entirely. Without copies like this one, we would have no idea what it looked like.

👑
Bust of Ramesses the Great
04Room 4 — Egyptian Sculpture
▾
Why It Matters
"This head is over 3,000 years old. The man it depicts ruled Egypt for 66 years and built more monuments than any other pharaoh."

Carved from two types of granite during the 19th Dynasty, around 1250 BC, the bust once formed part of a colossal seated statue of Ramesses II. The scale and quality of the carving reflects the power and ambition of one of Egypt's greatest rulers. The poet Shelley is thought to have been inspired by a description of this statue when he wrote Ozymandias; a poem about a long-forgotten ruler whose monument lies shattered in the desert. "Look on my works, ye Mighty, and despair."

⚱️
Mummy of Katebet
05Room 63 — Egyptian Mummies
▾
Why It Matters
"Katebet was a real person. She had a job title, a social role and a name that has survived 3,300 years."

Katebet was a Chantress of Amun; a singer who performed religious music in the temple of the god Amun, and her mummy dates to around 1330 to 1250 BC. The wrappings are exceptionally well preserved and the funerary mask and golden hands visible on the outer casing reflect her high social status. The museum has used non-invasive scanning to learn more about her without unwrapping her.

💬 Talking Point "Most people who have ever lived are completely unknown to history. Katebet is known because her society believed strongly enough in the afterlife to preserve her this carefully. What does that tell us about what ancient Egyptians valued?" The mummy gallery is often where students spend the most time, and the question of what a culture chooses to preserve says a great deal about what it believes.
⚔️
Samurai Armour
06Room 92 — Japan
▾
Why It Matters
"This armour was designed to be frightening, functional and beautiful simultaneously."

This full suit of Edo period armour belonged to a member of the Mori family and dates from between the 17th and 19th centuries. The lacquered plates, silk cords and dramatic horned helmet reflect both the practical requirements of combat and the deep symbolism of samurai culture. Every element including the colours, the materials and proportions, carried specific meaning about the wearer's rank, clan and values.

✨
Oxus Treasure
07Room 52 — Ancient Iran
▾
Why It Matters
"This collection of gold and silver objects was found by chance on a riverbank in the 1870s. It's one of the most important surviving collections from the ancient Persian Empire."

The Oxus Treasure is one of the museum's less obvious highlights; a hoard of gold and silver objects from the Achaemenid Persian Empire, dating from the 5th to the 3rd centuries BC. It includes jewellery, votive plaques, model chariots and ceremonial pieces that reveal the wealth, craftsmanship and religious practices of an empire that once stretched from Greece to India. The collection was found near the Oxus River (now the Amu Darya), reportedly by merchants who were then robbed, with pieces gradually recovered and eventually reaching the British Museum.

⛵
Sutton Hoo Ship Burial
08Room 41 — Sutton Hoo & Europe
▾
Why It Matters
"In 1939, a woman digging in her garden in Suffolk discovered an entire royal burial ship buried underground — with the treasure still inside."

Edith Pretty hired an archaeologist to investigate some mysterious mounds on her Suffolk estate in 1939. What they found was a 7th-century Anglo-Saxon royal ship burial, with the impression of the wooden vessel still visible in the soil and the treasure intact inside. Helmets, weapons, intricate gold jewellery, ceremonial objects and silver from Byzantium were all preserved together. It is one of the most important archaeological discoveries in British history. Edith Pretty donated the entire find to the nation, refusing any payment.

🗿
Hoa Hakananai'a
09Room 24 — Living & Dying
▾
Why It Matters
"Easter Island is one of the most remote inhabited places on Earth. This statue was carved there between 1000 and 1200 AD and carried across the Pacific by a Royal Navy ship in 1868."

Hoa Hakananai'a, whose name roughly translates as "stolen or hidden friend", is one of the most spiritually significant objects in the museum. The basalt figure represents an important ancestor in Rapa Nui culture and carries carved symbols on its back associated with the Birdman cult, a competitive ritual that determined the island's leader each year. The Rapa Nui people have formally requested its return. In 2018, the mayor of Easter Island came to London specifically to make that request in person at the museum.

💬 Talking Point "The debate about the Parthenon sculptures and the debate about Hoa Hakananai'a are the same debate. But the circumstances are different: one was removed by a British lord, one by the Royal Navy. Does that distinction matter? And does it make a difference that Easter Island is so remote that relatively few people would ever be able to see this object there?"
🎲
Royal Game of Ur
10Room 56 — Mesopotamia
▾
Why It Matters
"This is one of the world's oldest known board games, dating to around 2600 BC. We know the rules because they were written on a clay tablet."

The Royal Game of Ur was found in the Royal Cemetery at Ur in modern-day Iraq and is approximately 4,600 years old. It's a two-player race game, and its rules were decoded from a cuneiform tablet dating to 177 BC, meaning the game was still being played, and its rules still being written down, nearly 2,500 years after this board was made. A British Museum curator famously played the game against an artificial intelligence system, demonstrating that rules deciphered from an ancient clay tablet could still produce a working game today.
